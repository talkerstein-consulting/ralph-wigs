Section I

“Chanel, but it references Bnei Brak.”

Gitty's line is the whole strategy. It sounds like a contradiction. It isn't — because luxury houses already sell desire without showing skin. They do it through restraint, gesture, light and craft. That is exactly the bridge to a modest brand.

What we take from Chanel
  • One soft, directional light — shadow is the luxury signal, not flat brightness.
  • Vast negative space; the subject fills less than a third of the frame.
  • One colour world, repeated relentlessly, until it becomes the logo.
  • A serif wordmark, wide letter-spacing, and the confidence to under-explain.
What we take from Bnei Brak
  • Sensuality moves to the hands, the hair in motion, the gaze — never the body.
  • Coverage is absolute: high necks, collarbone-up, or no coverage question at all.
  • A back-of-head or profile reads as more editorial, not less.
  • Humility and discretion signal trust; boasting reads as cheap.

The single most Ralph image — a fall of waves in warm directional light, a hand setting the hair, in a quiet ivory frame — is at the same time the most Chanel and the most tznius picture we can make. That is the identity, not a compromise.

Section II

The one decision: real photography

You cannot build a $2,000–$6,000 sheitel brand on AI images. It is the one corner that cannot be cut — and here is why, for the owners.

  • Your buyer is the world's toughest wig critic. A frum woman spots a fake hairline, plastic shine or “helmet” density instantly — and AI gets exactly those things wrong.
  • Hair is the hardest thing for AI to fake. It's tens of thousands of strands and complex light-scatter; diffusion models only approximate it. Forensic deepfake detectors are literally built on the fact that AI hair flickers.
  • A fake image disproves the promise. Ralph's pitch is “undetectable, real hair.” An image that looks fake actively argues the opposite.
  • Real photography pays for itself. In beauty, real/UGC imagery lifts conversion up to 161%, video reviews +45%, ~$4 back per $1. It isn't a cost — it's the thing that makes the wig look worth the price.
NO-PASS · AI-generated
Note the too-perfect, uniform hairline and plastic sheen — the exact tells your customer catches. Fine for mood; fatal for the product shot.

The rule.  AI is welcome for mood, backgrounds and abstract texture. The actual wig, on a head, that a customer judges and buys — real photograph, and ideally video (hair in motion is the proof of realism).

Section III

The Visual System

Ten rules. If every asset obeys them, the brand looks expensive — automatically.

01 · Light

One soft source, ~45°, gently wrapping. Shadow on the far side. Never flat, even, ring-lit brightness — that is the #1 cheap tell.

02 · Space

The subject fills under 30% of the frame. Negative space is mandatory. Emptiness reads as confidence.

03 · Colour

Warm ivory / champagne / antique-gold for product. Reserve deep black-&-white for heritage and film only. Never over-saturate.

04 · Crop

Tight and partial — nape, profile, hands-in-hair, back-of-head — over full-frontal portraits.

05 · Sensuality

Carried by hands, hair movement, gaze and texture. Never by skin. This is the whole modest-luxury technique.

06 · Gaze

Three-quarter or downward, mysterious. Never a direct, smiling, “selling” look to camera in hero imagery.

07 · Type

A Didot-leaning serif wordmark, all-caps, wide letter-spacing. White space around type = 3× what feels enough.

08 · Restraint

No props beyond one symbolic object. No text on photos. No clutter. One focal point per frame.

09 · Motion

Brand film = slow, archival black-&-white, craftsman's hands. No fast cuts, no upbeat pop.

10 · Coverage

Absolute. High necklines, collarbone-up, or angles that never raise the question. No exceptions, ever.

Section V · The PASS Gallery
✓ Pass · The Signature
Back-of-head hero

The most Chanel and the most tznius shot at once.

A fall of glossy waves seen from behind, cream high-neck top, warm 45° light, ivory void. The hair is the entire subject — aspirational, sensual through movement, and it never raises a modesty question because there is no front to cover.

Why it passes: directional light, negative space, warm palette, partial crop, absolute coverage. This is the brand's default hero.

✓ Pass · The Gesture
Hands in hair

Sensuality lives in the hand.

A single elegant hand drawing through the waves — a delicate ring, warm light, no face. This is the modest-luxury technique borrowed straight from the jewellery houses: desire is carried by gesture and touch, not by the body.

Why it passes: tactile, intimate, entirely modest, and it proves softness — the thing a buyer wants to feel before she buys.

Texture macro

Close enough to see the quality.

Section VI · The NO-PASS Gallery
✕ No-Pass · Mannequin
The #1 cheap tell

A wig on a foam head sells inventory, not a house.

Styrofoam heads under flat store light are the single fastest way to look cheap. It reads as “stock,” not “fashion.” Even for faceless Israel-facing content, use a real head, a texture macro, or the atelier — never a mannequin.

Why it fails: no light, no life, no craft, no aspiration.

13
RALPH
✕ No-Pass · Plastic / Fake
Synthetic sheen & helmet shape

The shine that screams “wig.”

Uniform mirror-gloss, stiff helmet volume, a doll-like finish. Synthetic fibre reflects light in a flat plastic way that real hair never does — and hard flash makes it worse. This is precisely the look Ralph exists to beat.

Why it fails: plastic sheen, no movement, harsh flat flash, uncanny.

14
RALPH
✕ No-Pass · The Snapshot
Clutter & harsh flash

A phone snap in a messy room.

Busy background, on-camera flash, uneven light. It may be an authentic client photo — but authenticity without art direction reads as amateur, and amateur kills a luxury price. (Real-client content is powerful; it just has to be shot, not snapped.)

Why it fails: clutter, flat flash, no focal point, no palette.

RALPH
✕ No-Pass · Garish
Over-saturated, over-sharpened

Loud colour is the opposite of luxury.

Punchy orange/red, crushed contrast, e-commerce over-sharpening. Saturation reads as “discount.” Ralph's colour is warm, low, and quiet — the restraint is the price tag.

Why it fails: saturation, harsh contrast, cheap processing.

Section VII

The PASS List

If an image does these things, it's on-brand. Use it.

Craft & composition
  • One soft directional light; a real, gentle shadow.
  • Warm ivory / champagne palette (or B&W for heritage).
  • Generous negative space; one focal point.
  • Tight, partial crops — nape, profile, hands, back-of-head.
  • Real human hair with diffuse, matte, moving sheen.
  • Video of hair in motion wherever possible.
Modesty & tone
  • High necklines; collarbone-up when a face shows.
  • Sensuality via hands, movement, gaze, texture.
  • Three-quarter or downward gaze; quiet, not “selling.”
  • Long sleeves, modest wardrobe, elegant styling.
  • The maker, the craft, the atelier — always safe.
  • Discreet, humble, confident. Under-explained.

The NO-PASS List

If an image does any of these, it does not ship — no exceptions.

Looks cheap / looks fake
  • Mannequin / foam-head product shots.
  • Plastic synthetic sheen; stiff “helmet” hair.
  • AI-generated hair as the product shot (fake hairline, flicker).
  • Flat, even, ring-lit or hard on-camera flash.
  • Cluttered backgrounds; amateur phone snaps.
  • Over-saturated colour; over-sharpened e-commerce look.
  • Text over the photo; multiple focal points; logo clutter.
Breaks modesty (absolute)
  • Bare shoulders, décolletage, cleavage, bare arms.
  • Sleeveless, low, tight or clingy garments.
  • Any skin below the neckline.
  • Reclining or overtly sensual posing.
  • Direct, seductive eye-contact selling to camera.
  • Competitor names or marks in any prompt or asset.

These modesty failures are stated as rules, not illustrated — we hold ourselves to the same standard as the work.

Section VIII

The Shoot Brief

Hand this to the photographer. It turns the whole book into a day of shooting.

LightOne soft key at ~45°; a dedicated hair/rim light high and behind for the “halo” that gives strands dimension. Contain it with a grid — too much power blows out texture. Never flat or frontal flash.
Lens & settings90–105mm macro for strand detail (f/8–f/16 for sharpness across the strands); f/2.8–f/4 for portrait separation. Shoot tethered. Expect ~50 frames per look — hair never falls the same way twice.
PrepDry shampoo / texturizing spray to kill synthetic shine before shooting. Don't over-retouch flyaways in post — that creates fake “helmet hair.”
PaletteWarm ivory seamless for product; window-lit workshop for heritage (B&W). One world, held all day.
ModestyCollarbone-up or high necklines/long sleeves already styled in. Upright, confident poses. No coverage decisions left to the crop.

The Shot List

Hero stills
  • Back-of-head, voluminous waves, ivory void (the signature).
  • Three-quarter profile, high neck, rim-lit halo.
  • Crown / part top-down — proof of a realistic part.
  • ¾ back over-shoulder — nape blend, no cap edge.
Texture & detail (macro)
  • Strand-level crown; hairline / temple (baby hairs into skin).
  • Part-line; ends & tips (natural taper, not blunt).
Movement & video — the closers
  • Slow-mo hair flip on a head-turn.
  • Hand drawing through the hair, close crop.
  • Walk-away / turn-back, shoulders-up, modest.
  • Light wind for individual-strand flyaways.
Heritage & lifestyle
  • Atelier hands: combing, setting, knotting (B&W).
  • Before → process → after (locked camera, for trust).
  • Golden-hour ¾ (true daylight colour), modest wardrobe.
  • Vanity / mirror morning moment; Shabbos-table ¾.

Section IX

Two Tracks, One House

America and Israel genuinely conflict on what may be shown. Don't blend them — run one brand spine in two registers.

For US · Miami · Italy · Paris

The Editorial Track

Audience: Modern-Orthodox / Yeshivish buyers who want undetectable, styled, expensive hair.

Imagery: face-forward (collarbone-up), styled, aspirational — the profile & turtleneck heroes.

Voice: “Chanel” — aspirational, English (Yiddish for warmth).

Channel: Instagram, WhatsApp, the pop-up tour, trunk shows.

For Israel-Haredi · strict Hasidic

The Atelier Track

Audience: Bnei-Brak-coded, humility-first; markets where a woman's face can't be printed.

Imagery: faceless — back-of-head, hands, texture, the workshop. Old-world craft, not the wearer.

Voice: discreet, kavod-coded, Hebrew; no superlatives.

Channel: word-of-mouth, salon reputation, vetted lists, print-adjacent.

The gift: the faceless “Atelier” track isn't a compromise — luxury houses shoot faceless product all the time. It works everywhere, including the strictest channels. Test every Israel-facing asset against one question: “Would this pass in Yated?”

Section X

Benchmarks & Copy Hooks

What the market looks like
  • Emulate Shevy ($6,850+) — B&W, single-line taglines, product-as-hero. The most “Italian house” look out there.
  • Yaffa & BK Wigs prove real-model warmth + video (BK's 69K following) sells.
  • Two open lanes: nobody publishes a confident price ladder — own “openly tiered, confidently expensive.” And Ralph's 1966 + Italy heritage beats every rival but one.
  • Cheapest tell across the whole category: mannequin heads.
Copy hooks the research handed us
  • “Hand-tied, strand by strand.”
  • “100% European virgin human hair — never synthetic.”
  • “A part that moves. A hairline that isn't a straight line.”
  • “It moves like your own hair.” (→ why video closes.)
  • “Atelier-direct since 1966. Made in Italy.”
The things that make a wig look real are the same things that make Ralph worth the price. Photograph those, and the brand sells itself.
