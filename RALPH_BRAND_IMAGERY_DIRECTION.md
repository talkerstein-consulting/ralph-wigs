# RALPH — Brand & Imagery Direction
*Prepared for Gitty & the owners · 2026-06-30 · by Talkerstein*

> **The brief, in one line:** *Chanel-level polish, Bnei-Brak-level discretion.*
> Aspirational and expensive to the eye — modest, humble, and trustworthy to the soul.
> The luxury is in the **restraint**, not the skin.

---

## 0. The decision the owners need to make first: real photography

**You cannot build a $2,000–$6,000 sheitel brand on AI images. Full stop.**

Here's why this isn't Gitty being difficult — it's the single thing the money rides on:

- **Your buyer is the world's toughest wig critic.** A frum woman shopping a luxury sheitel can spot a fake hairline, plasticky shine, or "helmet" density from across a room. AI gets *exactly these things wrong* — uncanny faces, too-uniform density, glossy plastic sheen, hair that looks "pasted on," movement that doesn't read as real ([Power Your Curls](https://www.poweryourcurls.com/ai-generated-hair-and-beauty-standards-problems-and-data/)). For *this* audience, "looks a little off" = "looks cheap" = "not worth the price."
- **The product IS the realism.** Your whole pitch is "undetectable, looks like real hair, European virgin hair, made by hand since 1966." An AI image that looks fake actively *disproves your core promise.* It's the worst possible own-goal.
- **Where AI is fine (and we'll keep using it):** mood, backgrounds, abstract hair-texture, lighting studies, web placeholders while we shoot. **Where it's banned:** the actual wig, on a head, that a customer is meant to judge and buy.

**The owner conversation:** Skimping on imagery isn't saving money on a line item — it's saving money on *the thing that makes the wig look worth its price.* One real photo/video shoot is cheaper than the sales it loses every month it isn't there. **This is the one corner that cannot be cut.**

## 1. "Chanel but Bnei Brak" — what it actually means visually

It's **not** a contradiction. Luxury houses already sell desire without skin — through **restraint, gesture, light, and craft.** That's the bridge.

The codes that read EXPENSIVE (from Chanel, Van Cleef, Dior, Hermès):
- **One soft, directional light** (45°, window-style) — shadow on the other side. *Flat, even lighting is the #1 tell of cheap e-commerce.* Shadow = luxury.
- **Massive negative space.** Hero subject fills <30% of the frame. (Van Cleef's signature: the product is <20% of the image; light and emptiness do the rest.)
- **One color world, used relentlessly** — recommend **warm ivory / champagne / antique-gold** for product (flatters hair, says "Italian atelier"), reserving **slow B&W** for heritage/film only.
- **Tight, partial crops** — nape, profile, hands-in-hair, back-of-head — over full-frontal portraits.
- **Generous letter-spacing** on the wordmark, a **Didot-style serif** (couture, not tech), 3× the white space you think you need.
- **No props, no clutter, no text-on-photo, no salesy eye-contact-to-camera.**

The codes that read MODEST (Bnei Brak) — and how sensuality survives without skin:
- **Sensuality moves into the hands, the hair in motion, the gaze, and texture** — never the body. (Van Cleef literally builds desire through "the gestures of the hand." For us: fingers through strands, a hand lifting hair, a sheitel being set.)
- **Movement implies life** — hair caught mid-turn, a fall of waves — without removing an inch of coverage.
- **Profile / back-of-head / silhouette** reads as *more* editorial and mysterious, not less — and sidesteps the modesty decision entirely.
- **Coverage stays absolute:** collarbone-up only when a face shows; turtlenecks; never a neckline question.

> **The synthesis:** A back-of-head shot of glorious waves in warm directional light, hands setting the hair, in a quiet ivory frame, is *simultaneously* the most Chanel AND the most tznius image you can make. Lean into it as the brand's signature — it's not a compromise, it's the identity.

## 2. Why wigs look fake — and how to shoot them real

**The giveaways (kill these):**
- Lace/hairline that doesn't match skin tone (the #1 fake tell) ([Mandeville London](https://www.mandevillelondon.com/blog/ask-a-wigmaker-what-makes-a-wig-look-fake)).
- Plasticky **shine** under hard light/flash.
- **Too-uniform density** ear-to-ear — no real hairline is that perfect.
- **Helmet volume**, over-styled sameness, visible cap edge.

**How the best make it read real:**
- Customized, skin-matched lace; a **plucked gradient hairline** (thin at the edge, thicker in).
- **Soft natural/window light** that minimizes contrast and tames shine (the reason promo shots look better than real life).
- Show the **part and hairline done right**, baby hairs, realistic density.
- **VIDEO is the proof.** Hair *swishing, falling, turning* in slow motion is what convinces a skeptical buyer a sheitel looks real — stills can't do it alone. This is also pure modest-luxury vocabulary (movement = sensuality without skin).

### The real-photo / video shot list (hand to the photographer)
1. **The hero (signature):** back-of-head, voluminous waves, warm 45° directional light, ivory void. Modest by construction.
2. **Hands-in-hair macro:** fingers drawing through strands — texture + craft + sensuality.
3. **Profile:** jaw-to-crown, hair falling, soft shadow side.
4. **Hairline / part close-up:** proof of a natural, undetectable front.
5. **Texture macro:** light catching individual fibers (abstract, 100% modest, gorgeous).
6. **The atelier:** Ralph's hands working — combing, setting, knotting (your "since 1966" credibility, already shot in B&W).
7. **Motion clips (10–15s each):** hair turn, hair fall, a set being finished. Slow, no fast cuts, no pop music.
8. **On-client, collarbone-up,** turtleneck/high-neck, three-quarter gaze — for the few face-forward hero shots.

## 3. North America vs Israel — run TWO tracks, don't blend

The markets genuinely conflict. Serve both with **one brand spine, two creative registers:**

| | **"Editorial" track** (US / Italy / Paris) | **"Atelier" track** (Israel-Haredi / strict Hasidic) |
|---|---|---|
| **Audience** | Modern-Orthodox / Yeshivish / Miami / Boro-Park "hoyla" buyers who want *undetectable, styled, expensive-looking* hair | Bnei Brak-coded, humility-first; stricter enclaves where a woman's face can't even be printed |
| **Imagery** | Face-forward (collarbone-up), styled, aspirational | **Faceless:** back-of-head, hands, texture, the workshop. The "old-world atelier," not the wearer |
| **Language** | "Chanel" — aspirational, English (+ Yiddish for trust) | Humble, kavod-coded, discreet; Hebrew; **no superlatives/boasting** |
| **Channel** | Instagram, WhatsApp, trunk shows, pop-up tour | Word-of-mouth, salon reputation, print-adjacent, vetted WhatsApp lists; note "kosher phones" block parts of this market from digital entirely |

**Two strategic gifts from the research:**
- **The "atelier" faceless track isn't a workaround — it's premium.** Luxury houses shoot faceless product all the time. Position it as old-world craft, and it works *everywhere*, including the strictest channels.
- **Bnei Brak pedigree is a heritage flex.** Israel (Bnei Brak especially) is a global wig-making *manufacturing & training* hub — that artisanal provenance is a genuine luxury-origin story most US competitors (Shuly, Chavie Russell, etc.) can't authentically tell. Use it.

**Two things to confirm with the owners (research couldn't):** (1) Are the Miami/Paris Sephardi clients even wig-buyers, or tichel-buyers? (Sephardi poskim often forbid wigs.) (2) Exact price-tier the brand wants to anchor (Israel runs ~30–50% lower in USD than NY/Miami luxury).

## 4. What to do now

1. **Lock the decision:** real photo + video shoot for the actual product. AI stays for mood/backgrounds only. *(This is the owners' call — give them Section 0.)*
2. **Shoot the signature library** off the shot list (Section 2) — the back-of-head/hands/texture/atelier set covers BOTH tracks at once.
3. **Commission a Didot-leaning serif wordmark** + lock one warm-ivory color world. (Cheapest "looks expensive" lever.)
4. **Cut a "Since 1966" heritage film** — slow, B&W, Ralph's hands, no fast cuts. Differentiator nobody in the space has.
5. **Build two content registers** in the one Instagram/site spine (Editorial vs Atelier) and tag content by market.

## 6. Competitor teardown — Israel / Bnei Brak (luxury = restraint itself)

The establishment players: **Zahava Witzman** (Rabbi Tarfon St, Bnei Brak — ₪5,000–25,000; her **storefront sign is literally just her name, no slogan, no photo** — the ultimate "you already know who I am"; also runs the *Galit Italia* wig school), **Y&B / Yehudit Bergman**, **Ayala Wigs** ("boutique, handmade, each wig as art" — closest to the atelier framing), **TILLI'S** (one of the few with Instagram), **Chevi Montag**, **Ruchi Kashman**.

**The cultural hard constraint:** Bnei Brak has a **municipal bylaw against women's photos** on billboards; the strict Haredi press (e.g. *Yated Ne'eman*) won't even print an image that *looks like a woman wearing a wig.* So premium sheitel marketing there runs on **mannequin/back-of-head shots, hair-texture macros, and TEXT-LED prestige** — the macher's name, years, hair-provenance, "by appointment." Luxury is signaled by **reputation, scarcity of self-promotion, and hair pedigree** — not imagery.

**The white space:** *None* of the Israeli premium players has a polished **brand system** (logotype, type, packaging) on par with a Western luxury house. A "Chanel-but-Bnei-Brak"-correct identity — monogram/wordmark-led, gold/cream serif, hair-texture macro, zero faces — is genuinely unclaimed. (Pressure-test every Israel-facing asset against: *"Would this pass in Yated?"*)

## 7. The AI-imagery evidence (for the owners)

Not opinion — the hair industry is already saying this out loud:
- A hair brand's own journal: AI imagery *"has not responded to bleach or toner… is not affected by hair density, growth patterns or texture,"* so **real photos are required** for any claim about how a product truly looks ([Mosu Hair](https://www.mosu.hair/journal/insights/ai-in-the-hair-industry-why-real-photos-still-matter/)).
- Stylists now act as *"reality checkers"* against AI-fantasy hair clients bring in — ~half of one bridal firm's client inspiration is AI-generated and **unachievable** ([Axios](https://www.axios.com/2026/04/18/ai-generated-hair-makeup-inspo-pictures), [CBC](https://www.cbc.ca/news/canada/edmonton/ai-generated-images-creating-unrealistic-expectations-hairdressers-say-1.7378463)).
- **Video is the proof of realism:** motion forces hair to obey physics a fake can't fake — individual strand sway and bounce-back is the convincer ([Daniel Alain](https://www.danielalain.com/blogs/articles/the-most-realistic-looking-wigs)). Video also lifts engagement ~40% over stills.
- AI *is* great for backgrounds / mood / lifestyle scenery (up to 125% CTR lift) — just **not for the product-truth shot**.

**Why hair specifically is the thing AI can't fake** (the technical knockout punch for the owners): hair is *the* hardest object in computer vision — tens of thousands of interacting strands plus complex light-scattering that diffusion models only statistically approximate, never actually simulate. The documented failure modes are exactly a wig buyer's eye: AI renders hair as "broad strokes, not strands," can't do baby hairs/flyaways, produces a waxy "mannequin-dipped-in-wax" sheen, and **smears the boundary where hair meets skin/background** (the part, the hairline — the precise places a sheitel must be perfect). It's so unreliable that **forensic deepfake detectors are literally built on hair instability** — "flickering hair" is a known tell for synthetic video ([arXiv ControlHair](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2509.21541), [Which One is AI](https://whichoneis.ai/blog/how-to-spot-ai-generated-images), [CloudRetouch](https://www.cloudretouch.com/de-plasticize-ai-skin-texture-photoshop/)). If a $30B deepfake-detection field uses "AI can't do hair" as a *feature*, a wig brand cannot lean on AI for the product shot.

**And real photography pays for itself** (the budget argument, in numbers): 92% of consumers trust real people over branded ads; 86% say authenticity drives the purchase ([WiserNotify](https://wisernotify.com/blog/ugc-statistics/)). In beauty/cosmetics specifically: real-client/UGC content drives **up to 161% higher conversion**, product-page real imagery **~79% lift**, video reviews **45% higher conversion** ([Taggbox](https://taggbox.com/blog/ugc-for-cosmetics-brands/), [Asendia](https://www.asendia.com/asendia-insights/ugc-beauty-ecommerce-strategies)) — structured programs report ~**$4 back per $1 spent.** Before/after + real-client content functions as a *visual review*, which is exactly what a nervous $6,000 buyer needs. (Note: wig-*specific* conversion stats floating online trace to weak sources — don't cite those externally; the general-beauty numbers above are solid.)

## 8. The expanded shot list (give to the photographer)

**Lighting:** a hair-light high and behind (rim/backlight) for the "halo" that makes hair dimensional; one soft 45° key; expect ~50 frames per look (hair never falls the same twice). **Lens:** 90–105mm macro for strand detail (f/8–f/16); f/2.8–f/4 for portrait separation.

- **Hero stills:** front ¾ (proves hairline/face-frame) · crown/part top-down (proves realistic part + density) · profile silhouette on rim light (proves it's not a helmet) · ¾ back over-shoulder (proves nape blend, no cap edge).
- **Texture macro:** strand-level crown · hairline/temple (baby hairs into skin) · part-line · ends/tips (natural taper, not blunt synthetic).
- **Movement/video (the closers):** slow-mo hair flip on a head-turn · hand-through-hair close crop · walk-away/turn-back (shoulders-up, modest) · light wind-machine for flyaways.
- **Before/after:** locked camera+light+angle, base vs styled · process triptych (cap-on → mid → finished) for trust.
- **Lifestyle (modest):** golden-hour ¾ (true daylight color) · vanity/mirror morning moment · Shabbos-table/simcha seated ¾ · full-length walking shot, hair as focal point.
- *All framing: collarbone-up or high necklines/elbow coverage, upright confident poses — never reclining or sensualized.*

## 9. Copy hooks the research handed us (for site + sales)

The "fake tells" research doubles as **proof points** for premium copy — the things that make a wig look real are exactly Ralph's selling points:
- **"Hand-tied, strand by strand"** — the realism baseline; a hand-tied monofilament part is what makes hair look like it grows from the scalp.
- **"100% European virgin human hair — never synthetic."** Real reason it matters, in plain terms: synthetic fiber reflects light in a uniform *plasticky gloss*; human hair's cuticle scatters light into a soft *matte sheen*. That's the difference the eye reads as "real."
- **"A part that moves, a hairline that isn't a straight line."** Natural irregularity = the craft. (Cheap wigs give themselves away with too-perfect, too-uniform, too-dense hairlines.)
- **"It moves like your own hair."** Movement is the proof — which is why **video** is the closer.
- Real-world reference brands doing "real people / lived-in" well (not for copying, for showing the photographer the vibe): K18's "Real People Real Results," lived-in balayage styling (undone, light-led texture). Pre-shoot tech note for the photographer: **dry shampoo / texturizing spray to kill synthetic shine**, a raking hair-light for texture, and *don't* over-retouch flyaways (that's what creates fake "helmet hair" in post).
